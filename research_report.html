Agent-Based Modeling of Digital Stress Propagation in Social Networks: An Empirical Investigation of Targeted Intervention Strategies

Amit Verma, Jayanth Reddy Y, Ishani Singh, Tanvi Ganesh Borkar, Yashika Gupta

Department of Computer Science and Engineering
[Institution Name], November 2025

ABSTRACT

Digital stress and burnout among students have emerged as critical mental health challenges, yet traditional intervention approaches treat stress as an individual phenomenon rather than a network-level contagion. We present an agent-based simulation framework that models stress propagation through social networks using empirically grounded behavioral data from 700 smartphone users. Our model incorporates homophilous network structures (assortativity = 0.426), stress contagion dynamics calibrated from social epidemiology literature, and self-reinforcing feedback loops that capture doomscrolling behavior. We evaluate a targeted intervention strategy where the top 5% of highly connected individuals reduce their digital usage and stress transmission. Results demonstrate a 5.9% reduction in network-wide stress (p < 0.05) but reveal a paradox: targeting influencers minimizes average stress while increasing burnout cases by 12.5% compared to targeting isolated individuals. Additional experiments show that collective action (universal participation) achieves 5× greater stress reduction than targeted approaches, and that intervention effectiveness varies significantly with network conformity levels. These findings suggest that optimal digital wellness strategies require hybrid approaches that balance network leverage with protection of vulnerable individuals.

Keywords: agent-based modeling, social contagion, digital stress, network interventions, mental health

1. INTRODUCTION

The pervasive integration of digital technologies into daily life has created new challenges for mental health and wellbeing, particularly among young adults and students. Smartphone usage patterns show strong correlations with stress, anxiety, and sleep disturbances (Thomée et al., 2011). However, most research treats digital stress as an individual-level problem, overlooking the potential for stress to propagate through social networks via peer influence and social comparison mechanisms.

Social contagion research has demonstrated that emotional states, including happiness and depression, spread through interpersonal networks with measurable transmission rates (Christakis & Fowler, 2008; Rosenquist et al., 2011). If digital stress exhibits similar contagion dynamics, then intervention strategies could potentially leverage network structure to amplify their impact. Specifically, targeting highly connected individuals (influencers) might reduce population-level stress more efficiently than random or universal interventions.

This research addresses three questions: (1) Can agent-based modeling effectively simulate stress contagion dynamics using empirically grounded behavioral data? (2) Do targeted interventions on network influencers produce measurable reductions in population-level stress? (3) What are the trade-offs between different intervention strategies in terms of average outcomes versus worst-case scenarios?

We developed a computational model that simulates 100 synthetic agents interacting over 30 days in a homophilous social network. Agent behaviors are statistically derived from the Valakhorasani User Behavior Dataset (700 smartphone users), and stress dynamics incorporate established parameters from social contagion literature. Our intervention simulations test the hypothesis that reducing usage and transmission among the top 5 most connected individuals will decrease network-wide stress levels.

2. METHODOLOGY

2.1 Dataset and Persona Definition

We utilized the Valakhorasani User Behavior Dataset (2024) comprising 700 smartphone users with 11 behavioral attributes including screen time, application usage frequency, notification counts, and battery consumption. Initial profiling revealed strong correlations between screen time and app usage (r = 0.89) and battery drain (r = 0.76), validating the dataset's internal consistency.

To capture behavioral heterogeneity, we applied k-means clustering (k = 5) on six normalized features: screen time, app usage, battery drain, app installations, app uninstallations, and notification frequency. The silhouette score (0.524) confirmed adequate cluster separation. This analysis identified five distinct user personas (Table 1).

2.2 Agent Generation and Validation

We generated 100 synthetic agents through stratified sampling from persona distributions, maintaining the population proportions observed in the original dataset. Each agent received:

  • Baseline behavioral attributes sampled from their persona's normal distribution (screen time, app usage, battery drain)
  • Initial stress level calculated as: S0 = (behavior_class - 1) / 4 × 100, scaling linearly from 0-100
  • Resilience coefficient drawn from Uniform(0.7, 1.3), representing individual stress resistance
  • Susceptibility coefficient from Uniform(0.5, 1.5), capturing variation in peer influence sensitivity
  • Variability coefficient from Uniform(0.8, 1.2), modeling behavioral consistency

Validation confirmed synthetic agents matched the original dataset within 5% error across all behavioral metrics, ensuring empirical grounding of the simulation.

2.3 Network Topology Construction

We constructed a homophilous social network using a Stochastic Block Model with persona-based connection probabilities:

  • P(connection | same persona) = 0.15
  • P(connection | different persona) = 0.03
  • P(connection | random bridge) = 0.01

This approach enforces homophily (the tendency for similar individuals to associate) observed in real social networks. The resulting network contained 100 nodes and 270 edges (mean degree = 5.4), with an assortativity coefficient of 0.426, confirming successful homophilous clustering (Newman, 2002). For comparison, random networks exhibit assortativity near 0.

Influencers were identified using degree centrality, selecting the top 5 agents with connection counts ranging from 13-18 (compared to network mean of 5.4). This 5% intervention coverage represents a realistic resource allocation for targeted programs.

2.4 Temporal Dynamics Model

The simulation executes daily updates over 30 days using two coupled equations that model stress contagion and behavioral feedback:

Stress Update (Network Contagion):

Si,t = α · Ui,tnorm + β · S̄neighbors,t-1 · χi - γ · ρi · 10

Where Si,t is agent i's stress at time t, Ui,tnorm is normalized usage (capped at 100), S̄neighbors,t-1 is the mean neighbor stress from the previous day, χi is agent i's susceptibility, and ρi is resilience. Parameters α = 0.6, β = 0.3, and γ = 0.1 represent relative weights of personal usage, peer influence, and resilience respectively.

Usage Update (Self-Reinforcing Feedback):

Ui,t = Ui,t-1 · (1 + δ · Si,t-1/100) · νi · εt

Where δ = 0.02 is the self-feedback rate (stress increases usage by 2% per 100 stress points), νi is agent-specific variability, and εt ~ N(1.0, 0.1) is daily stochastic noise.

2.5 Parameter Justification

Model parameters were calibrated from peer-reviewed literature:

  • β = 0.3: Christakis & Fowler (2008) observed ~30% transmission rate for happiness in the Framingham Heart Study social network
  • δ = 0.02: Thomée et al. (2011) documented 2-3% increase in phone usage among stressed young adults
  • Resilience range [0.7, 1.3]: Standard ±30% variance for personality traits in agent-based models (Epstein & Axtell, 1996)
  • Susceptibility range [0.5, 1.5]: Broader ±50% variance reflecting greater individual differences in social conformity

2.6 Intervention Design

We implemented two experimental scenarios, each replicated 5 times with different random seeds (42-46) to quantify variance:

Scenario A (Baseline): Natural evolution without intervention for 30 days.

Scenario B (Influencer Intervention): On day 10, the top 5 agents by degree centrality undergo simultaneous interventions:

  1. Usage clamping: Uinfluencer,t = 0.5 · Ucalculated,t
  2. Transmission dampening: Stress broadcast to neighbors multiplied by 0.3 (70% reduction)

The intervention timing (day 10 of 30) represents early response after stress accumulation but with sufficient remaining time to observe effects. The 70% transmission reduction models reduced social media posting and consequent stress exposure for neighbors.

2.7 Statistical Analysis

We computed mean and standard error across 5 runs for each scenario. Effect sizes were calculated as (Baseline - Intervention) / Baseline × 100%. Paired t-tests assessed statistical significance of differences, with significance threshold p < 0.05. Burnout was operationally defined as sustained stress > 80 (top 20% of stress scale).

Targeting the top 5% of connected individuals produced statistically significant reductions in final mean stress (p = 0.041) and accelerated system stabilization by 1.6 days (p = 0.038). 3.2 The Targeting Paradox

To investigate targeting strategy effects, we compared three intervention approaches using identical intervention mechanisms but different target selection criteria (Table 3).

A paradoxical relationship emerged: targeting influencers optimized average stress but worsened burnout outcomes (+12.5% compared to targeting isolated agents), while targeting isolated individuals minimized burnout despite higher average stress. This divergence suggests that network centrality creates dual effects: influencers broadcast stress widely (negative) but also serve as social support hubs (positive). When influencers reduce activity, stress propagation decreases network-wide, but their dependent neighbors experience localized stress spikes from lost social connection, pushing vulnerable individuals past the burnout threshold.

3.3 Collective Action Effectiveness

We evaluated a universal participation scenario where all 100 agents simultaneously reduce usage by 50% on a single day, maintaining the reduction for the remaining simulation period. This "digital detox" intervention achieved dramatically superior outcomes:

  • Final stress: 30.20 (29.1% reduction vs baseline)
  • Burnout cases: 0.6 (89.7% reduction vs baseline)

Collective action outperformed targeted intervention by approximately 5-fold, indicating that when peer influence is moderate (β = 0.3), universal behavior change dominates network leverage effects.

This suggests that optimal intervention strategies are context-dependent rather than universally applicable.

4. DISCUSSION

4.1 Interpretation of Main Findings

Our primary result - that targeting 5% of highly connected individuals reduces network stress by 5.9% - validates the hypothesis that digital stress exhibits measurable contagion dynamics amenable to network-based interventions. The effect size, while modest, represents meaningful impact given the minimal intervention coverage. Extrapolating to a campus of 1,000 students, identifying and supporting 50 key individuals could reduce stress for hundreds.

However, the targeting paradox reveals critical limitations of influence-maximization approaches. Optimizing for average outcomes can worsen tail risk (burnout), creating an equity-efficiency trade-off. This finding parallels recent critiques of targeted interventions in public health, where focusing on high-risk individuals may neglect systemic factors affecting vulnerable populations (Frohlich & Potvin, 2008).

4.2 Mechanisms Underlying the Paradox

We attribute the paradox to dual roles of network hubs. Influencers act as stress amplifiers (broadcasting to many neighbors) but simultaneously as social infrastructure (providing connection and support). Intervention creates asymmetric effects:

  • Positive network effect: Reduced broadcasting lowers average stress (first-order effect)
  • Negative support loss: Neighbors lose social connection, increasing individual vulnerability (second-order effect)

The negative effect concentrates among highly dependent individuals who cross the burnout threshold. This mechanism suggests that interventions should couple usage reduction with maintained social engagement - a nuance not captured in simple "digital detox" approaches.

4.3 Implications for Campus Wellness Programs

Our findings suggest three design principles for digital wellness interventions:

1. Hybrid Targeting: Combine network analysis to identify influencers with vulnerability screening to protect at-risk individuals. Monitor both average metrics and tail outcomes.

2. Context-Aware Strategies: Intervention design should consider population conformity levels. In echo chamber environments (dormitories, tight-knit majors), targeting influencers maximizes impact. In diverse, loosely connected populations, collective campaigns prove more effective.

3. Preserve Social Infrastructure: When intervening on central individuals, maintain their social connectivity while reducing stress broadcasting. For example, encourage synchronous activities (video calls) over asynchronous stress sharing (venting posts).

4.4 Limitations and Future Directions

Several limitations constrain interpretation. First, stress dynamics are theoretical constructs calibrated from literature rather than direct measurements of student stress propagation. Validation requires longitudinal Experience Sampling Method (ESM) data tracking actual stress contagion in student networks.

Second, our static network assumption ignores dynamic friendship formation and dissolution. Real social networks exhibit temporal evolution that could amplify or dampen intervention effects. Future work should incorporate dynamic network models where connections change based on stress similarity or intervention participation.

Third, we model stress transmission as linear, but real contagion may exhibit threshold effects (complex contagion) where multiple reinforcing exposures are required. Testing non-linear transmission functions could reveal conditions where targeting strategies become more or less effective.

Fourth, all simulations use a single network topology (seed=42). While we vary temporal stochasticity across 5 seeds, structural robustness requires testing across multiple network realizations with different clustering patterns and degree distributions.

Finally, our intervention represents an idealized compliance scenario. Real-world adoption requires addressing motivation, sustainability, and potential backlash effects not modeled here.

4.5 Broader Implications for Social Computing

This work demonstrates that agent-based modeling can bridge individual psychology and network dynamics, providing a computational laboratory for testing intervention hypotheses before deployment. The targeting paradox illustrates how network interventions can produce counterintuitive emergent effects not predictable from individual-level analysis alone.

More broadly, our findings contribute to ongoing debates about optimal intervention granularity in social systems. The dramatic superiority of collective action (5× better than targeted) when peer influence is moderate suggests that for many behavioral health challenges, population-wide approaches may outperform clever optimization. This echoes Rose's "prevention paradox" in epidemiology: small changes across everyone often beats large changes in high-risk subgroups.

However, the context-dependence we observed (β = 0.8 reverses this relationship) indicates no universal strategy. Effective intervention design requires understanding both network topology and behavioral coupling strength - a combination of structural and psychological analysis.

5. CONCLUSIONS

We developed and validated an agent-based model of digital stress propagation that combines empirical behavioral data with theoretical contagion dynamics. Our simulations demonstrate that targeting network influencers produces measurable stress reduction (5.9%, p < 0.05) but reveals a fundamental trade-off: optimizing average outcomes can worsen extreme cases. Collective action interventions substantially outperform targeted approaches when peer influence is moderate, but targeting becomes highly effective in conformist environments.

These findings suggest that effective digital wellness programs require hybrid strategies that leverage network structure while protecting vulnerable individuals, with specific design choices contingent on population context. Future work should validate contagion parameters using real longitudinal data, test dynamic network models, and investigate non-linear transmission mechanisms.

The broader contribution of this research lies in demonstrating how computational social science methods can reveal non-obvious emergent effects in social systems, providing evidence to guide resource allocation in mental health interventions. As digital technologies continue to reshape social interaction, network-aware approaches to wellbeing become increasingly necessary.
