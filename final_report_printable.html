1. The Core Idea

During exam season, we noticed stress spreading through friend groups like a virus. When one person posted about exam anxiety, suddenly everyone felt it. This made us wonder: if stress spreads through social networks, can we use disease control strategies to reduce it?

Our Question: If we identify the most well-connected people and help them reduce stress and screen time, would that reduce stress for everyone else too? Like vaccination - you don't need to vaccinate everyone if you vaccinate the right people.

2. The Dataset

We used the Valakhorasani User Behavior Dataset from Kaggle with 700 real smartphone users. The data included screen time, app usage, notifications, and battery drain.

Finding Five User Types

We ran K-Means clustering and found five distinct personas:

User Type Daily Screen Time % of People
Minimalists ~1.5 hours 14%
Moderate Users ~3 hours 21%
Active Users ~5 hours 31%
Heavy Users ~7 hours 25%
Digital Addicts ~10 hours 9%

3. Building the Simulation

100 Synthetic Agents

We generated 100 agents by sampling from our persona distributions. Each agent got:

  • Baseline screen time (from their persona)
  • Starting stress level
  • Resilience score (0.7-1.3) - some handle stress better
  • Susceptibility score (0.5-1.5) - how influenced by friends
  • Variability score (0.8-1.2) - daily habit consistency

The Social Network

We built a network with homophily (similar people connect more):

  • Same persona: 15% connection chance
  • Different persona: 3% connection chance
  • Random bridges: 1% for diversity

Result: 100 people, 270 friendships, average 5-6 friends per person. Assortativity = 0.426 confirmed strong clustering.

How Stress Spreads

We created two equations based on research papers:

Daily Stress Calculation:

Stress = 0.6 × (Phone Usage) + 0.3 × (Friend Stress) × (Susceptibility) - 0.1 × (Resilience)

60% from your behavior, 30% from friends (Christakis & Fowler 2008), 10% protective buffer.

Feedback Loop (Doomscrolling):

Today Usage = Yesterday Usage × (1 + 0.02 × Yesterday Stress/100) × Variability × Noise

When stressed, you use your phone more (2% increase per 100 stress points, from Thomée 2011), creating a vicious cycle.

4. The Intervention

We tested two scenarios, each 5 times with different random seeds:

Baseline: No intervention, just natural behavior for 30 days

Intervention: On Day 10:

  • Identify top 5 most connected people
  • Force their screen time to 50% of normal
  • Reduce their stress broadcasting by 70%

Why Day 10? Stress has built up enough to matter, but enough time remains to see effects.

Why 70% reduction? When someone uses their phone less, they also post less, so friends see less of their stress.

5. Results

Main Finding: Targeting 5 people (5% of network) reduced everyone's stress by 5.9%

Metric Baseline Intervention Change
Average Stress 42.61 ± 1.16 40.09 ± 0.73 -5.9%
Burnout Cases 5.8 ± 1.3 5.4 ± 1.1 -0.4 people
Days to Stabilize 23.4 21.8 1.6 days faster

The Targeting Paradox

We tested three targeting strategies:

Strategy Final Stress Burnout Cases
Target Influencers 40.09 (BEST) 3.60 (WORST)
Target Random 41.00 3.40
Target Isolated 41.62 (WORST) 3.20 (BEST)

The Paradox: Targeting influencers gives lowest average stress BUT highest burnout cases. Why? When you "quarantine" influencers:

  • Good: They stop spreading stress to 15+ friends
  • Bad: Those friends lose an important social connection, causing stress spikes in vulnerable people

It's like removing the popular kid from school. They spread some stress, but they're also a social hub. When they disappear, everyone connected feels the loss.

Digital Detox Day

What if all 100 people participate instead of just 5?

  • Stress drops by 29% (vs 6% for influencers)
  • Burnout cases drop to 0.6 (vs 5.4 baseline)

Collective action destroys targeted intervention. But getting 100% participation is way harder than convincing 5 people.

6. What We Learned

Networks aren't intuitive. The targeting paradox showed that the most connected people aren't always the best targets. It depends on whether you care about averages or preventing worst cases.

Collective beats targeted. When peer influence is moderate (30%), getting everyone to change a little beats targeting influencers heavily. But in echo chambers (high conformity), targeting influencers becomes super effective.

Feedback loops matter. The doomscrolling cycle (stress causes more usage causes more stress) is subtle but powerful over 30 days.

7. Problems We Ran Into

Seed dependency: Our network was randomly generated but we didn't lock the seed initially. Fixed by documenting that all results use seed=42.

Parameter choices: Values like resilience range [0.7, 1.3] were somewhat arbitrary. We used research papers where possible (0.3 from Christakis & Fowler, 0.02 from Thomée) but some choices were educated guesses.

Validation limits: We validated synthetic agents against real usage data (<5% error) but couldn't validate stress dynamics since we don't have real longitudinal stress data.

8. Real-World Applications

For campus wellness programs:

  • Don't rely only on targeting influencers - it helps average stress but can increase burnout
  • Collective campaigns (Digital Detox Wednesday) work better than targeting 25 key students
  • Hybrid is best: target influencers AND run population-wide campaigns
  • Late interventions still work - even Week 12 of semester shows effects

9. Limitations

  • It's a simulation, not real student stress data
  • Static network - friendships don't change over 30 days
  • Linear stress transmission - real contagion might have thresholds
  • Binary burnout (stress > 80) - real burnout is a spectrum
  • One network topology - would results generalize?

10. Implementation

Seven Python scripts running in sequence:

  1. data_profiling.py - Explore 700-user dataset
  2. persona_definition.py - K-means clustering for 5 types
  3. agent_factory.py - Generate 100 synthetic agents
  4. network_topology.py - Build social network
  5. temporal_engine.py - Run 30-day baseline
  6. run_scenarios.py - Run interventions (5 times each)
  7. create_visualizations.py - Create poster graphs

Total runtime: ~60 seconds. Used NetworkX, scikit-learn, pandas, numpy.

Conclusions

This project showed that stress operates at the network level, not just individually. Your stress affects friends, which affects their friends, which comes back to you.

The targeting paradox was our biggest surprise. You can't just target influencers and call it done - you need to think about how removing central nodes affects people who depend on them.

Most important lesson: collective action beats clever optimization. Getting everyone to participate even a little is more effective than getting key people to change a lot. But collective action is hard, so hybrid strategies (network analysis + population campaigns) probably work best.
