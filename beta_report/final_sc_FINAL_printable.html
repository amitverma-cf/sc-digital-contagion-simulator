Simulating Digital Stress Contagion in Social Networks: Testing Targeted Intervention Strategies

Amit Verma, Jayanth Reddy Yerabolu, Ishani Singh, Tanvi Ganesh Borkar, Yashika Gupta

Department of Computer Science and Engineering
Mahindra University, November 2025

ABSTRACT

Digital stress and burnout among students have become significant concerns related to technology use and wellbeing, but traditional approaches treat stress as an individual issue rather than a network-level contagion. We built an agent-based simulation that models the spread of digital stress through social networks using real behavioral data from 700 smartphone users. Our model incorporates homophilous network structures (assortativity = 0.426), stress propagation dynamics informed by social contagion research, and feedback loops that capture usage reinforcement behavior such as doomscrolling. We tested an intervention where the top 5% most connected individuals reduce their phone usage and stress transmission. Results show a 5.9% reduction in overall stress (p < 0.05), but reveal a paradox: targeting highly connected individuals lowers average stress while increasing burnout cases by 12.5% compared to targeting less connected individuals. Additional experiments show that universal participation achieves approximately five times greater effectiveness than targeted interventions, and that intervention outcomes depend on the level of social conformity within the network. These findings suggest that effective digital wellness strategies require a combination of network-aware targeting and population-level approaches, while accounting for vulnerable individuals.

1. INTRODUCTION

Digital technologies have become deeply integrated into daily life, creating new challenges related to technology use and wellbeing among young adults and students. Research shows that smartphone usage patterns correlate strongly with stress, anxiety, and sleep disturbances (Thomée et al., 2011). However, most existing research treats digital stress as an individual phenomenon, without accounting for how it may propagate through social networks via peer influence and social comparison.

Work on social contagion has shown that emotional states such as happiness and depression can spread through social networks at measurable rates (Christakis & Fowler, 2008; Rosenquist et al., 2011). While these studies focus on emotional states, they provide a conceptual foundation for modeling the spread of behaviorally driven phenomena such as digital stress. If digital stress follows similar propagation patterns, then intervention strategies could leverage network structure to amplify their effects. In particular, targeting highly connected individuals may reduce overall stress more efficiently than random or universal interventions.

In this study, the term "stress" refers specifically to digital stress and fatigue arising from patterns of smartphone usage and peer interaction. It does not represent clinical or emotional stress, but rather a behavioral proxy derived from observable digital activity.

This research addresses three questions: (1) Can agent-based modeling effectively simulate stress contagion dynamics using empirically grounded behavioral data? (2) Do targeted interventions on network influencers produce measurable reductions in population-level stress? (3) What are the trade-offs between different intervention strategies in terms of average outcomes versus worst-case scenarios?

We developed a computational model that simulates 100 agents interacting over 30 days in a homophilous social network. Agent behaviors are derived from the Valakhorasani User Behavior Dataset (700 smartphone users), and stress contagion dynamics incorporate parameters from social contagion research. Our simulations test whether reducing usage among the top 5 most connected individuals will decrease network-wide stress levels.

2. METHODOLOGY

2.1 Dataset and Persona Definition

We analyzed the Valakhorasani User Behavior Dataset containing 700 users with behavioral attributes. To create user personas, we applied k-means clustering on five continuous behavioral features: App Usage Time (min/day), Screen On Time (hours/day), Battery Drain (mAh/day), Number of Apps Installed, and Data Usage (MB/day). Features were standardized using StandardScaler normalization.

We evaluated cluster solutions from k=2 to k=8. The elbow method and silhouette analysis both indicated k=5 as optimal. At k=5, the silhouette score was 0.6435, indicating good cluster separation and cohesion. This selection yielded five distinct personas: Minimalist (low usage), Moderate User (medium usage), Active User (high usage), Heavy User (very high usage), and Digital Addict (intensive usage).

Correlations Between Behavioral Features:

Analysis of feature correlations revealed high covariance among behavioral metrics. Pearson correlation between App Usage Time and Screen On Time was r = 0.950. Correlation between Screen On Time and Battery Drain was r = 0.950. These high correlations indicate that behavioral features scale together, suggesting they measure a common underlying construct of overall device usage intensity.

2.2 Agent Generation and Validation

We created 100 fake agents by sampling from our persona groups, keeping the same proportions we saw in the original dataset. Each agent got:

  • Baseline behavioral attributes sampled from their persona's normal distribution (screen time, app usage, battery drain)
  • Initial stress level calculated as: S0 = (behavior_class - 1) / 4 × 100, scaling linearly from 0-100
  • Resilience coefficient drawn from Uniform(0.7, 1.3), representing individual stress resistance
  • Susceptibility coefficient from Uniform(0.5, 1.5), capturing variation in peer influence sensitivity
  • Variability coefficient from Uniform(0.8, 1.2), modeling behavioral consistency

Validation confirmed synthetic agents matched the original dataset within 5% error across all behavioral metrics, ensuring empirical grounding of the simulation.

2.3 Network Topology Construction

We constructed a homophilous social network using a Stochastic Block Model with persona-based connection probabilities:

  • P(connection | same persona) = 0.15
  • P(connection | different persona) = 0.03
  • P(connection | random bridge) = 0.01

This approach enforces homophily (the tendency for similar individuals to associate) observed in real social networks. The resulting network contained 100 nodes and 270 edges (mean degree = 5.4), with clustering coefficient of 0.080. The assortativity coefficient (0.426) confirms that agents preferentially connect with similar users, showing moderate homophilous mixing by persona despite the relatively sparse network structure.

Influencers were identified using degree centrality, selecting the top 5 agents with connection counts ranging from 10-12 (compared to network mean of 5.4). These agents (IDs: 32, 85, 78, 59, 66) with degrees 12, 11, 10, 10, and 11 respectively represent the 5% intervention coverage, which is a realistic resource allocation for targeted programs.

2.4 Temporal Dynamics Model

The simulation runs daily updates over 30 days using two coupled equations that model stress contagion and behavioral feedback:

Stress Update (Network Contagion):

Si,t = α · Ui,tnorm + β · S̄neighbors,t-1 · χi - γ · ρi · 10

Where Si,t is agent i's stress at time t, Ui,tnorm is normalized usage (capped at 100), S̄neighbors,t-1 is the mean neighbor stress from the previous day, χi is agent i's susceptibility, and ρi is resilience. Parameters α, β,and γ represent relative weights of personal usage, peer influence, and resilience.

Usage Update (Self-Reinforcing Feedback):

Ui,t = Ui,t-1 · (1 + δ · Si,t-1/100) · νi · εt

Where δ = 0.02 is the self-feedback rate (stress increases usage by 2% per 100 stress points), νi is agent-specific variability, and εt ~ N(1.0, 0.1) is daily stochastic noise.

2.5 Parameter Justification

We set model parameters based on published research papers:

  • β = 0.3: Peer influence weight calibrated to represent moderate network conformity, where neighbors contribute roughly 30% to individual stress dynamics
  • δ = 0.02: Thomée et al. (2011) documented 2-3% increase in phone usage among stressed young adults
  • Resilience range [0.7, 1.3]: Standard ±30% variance for personality traits in agent-based models (Epstein & Axtell, 1996)
  • Susceptibility range [0.5, 1.5]: Broader ±50% variance reflecting greater individual differences in social conformity

2.6 Intervention Design

We implemented two experimental scenarios, each replicated 5 times with different random seeds (42-46) to quantify variance:

Scenario A (Baseline): Natural evolution without intervention for 30 days.

Scenario B (Influencer Intervention): On day 10, the top 5 agents by degree centrality undergo simultaneous interventions:

  1. Usage clamping: Uinfluencer,t = 0.5 · Ucalculated,t
  2. Transmission dampening: Stress broadcast to neighbors multiplied by 0.3 (70% reduction)

The intervention timing (day 10 of 30) represents early response after stress accumulation but with sufficient remaining time to observe effects. The 70% transmission reduction models reduced social media posting and consequent stress exposure for neighbors.

2.7 Statistical Analysis

We computed mean and standard error across 5 runs for each scenario. Effect sizes were calculated as (Baseline - Intervention) / Baseline × 100%. We used paired t-tests to check if differences were statistically significant (p < 0.05). We defined burnout as stress above 80 (top 20% of the stress scale).

Targeting the top 5% of connected individuals produced statistically significant reductions in final mean stress (p = 0.041) and accelerated system stabilization by 1.6 days (p = 0.038). The total stress area under the curve decreased by 3.4% (p = 0.029), indicating sustained effects throughout the post-intervention period. However, burnout case reduction (-0.4 agents) did not reach statistical significance (p = 0.112).

3.2 The Targeting Paradox

To investigate targeting strategy effects, we compared three intervention approaches using identical intervention mechanisms but different target selection criteria (Table 3).

Strategy Avg. Degree Final Stress Burnout Cases
Target Influencers 10.8 40.09 (Best) 3.60 (Worst)

Target Isolated 2.1 41.55 (Worst) 3.20 (Best)

A paradoxical relationship emerged: targeting influencers optimized average stress but worsened burnout outcomes (+12.5% compared to targeting isolated agents), while targeting isolated individuals minimized burnout despite higher average stress. This divergence suggests that network centrality creates dual effects: influencers broadcast stress widely (negative) but also serve as social support hubs (positive). When influencers reduce activity, stress propagation decreases network-wide, but their dependent neighbors experience localized stress spikes from lost social connection, pushing vulnerable individuals past the burnout threshold.

3.3 Collective Action Effectiveness

We evaluated a universal participation scenario where all 100 agents simultaneously reduce usage by 50% on a single day, maintaining the reduction for the remaining simulation period. This "digital detox" intervention achieved dramatically superior outcomes:

  • Final stress: 30.20 (29.1% reduction vs baseline)
  • Burnout cases: 0.6 (85.0% reduction vs baseline)

Collective action outperformed targeted intervention by approximately 5-fold, indicating that when peer influence is moderate (β = 0.3), universal behavior change dominates network leverage effects.

Intervention effectiveness scales with conformity. In echo chamber environments (β = 0.8, typical of highly conformist settings like high schools), targeting influencers achieves 22% stress reduction with only 1 burnout case. This suggests that optimal intervention strategies are context-dependent rather than universally applicable.

4. DISCUSSION

4.1 Interpretation of Main Findings

Our main finding is that targeting 5% of highly connected people reduces network stress by 5.9%. This supports our idea that digital stress spreads through networks in ways we can measure and address. While the effect is modest, it's meaningful given that we only worked with 5% of the population. This suggests potential scalability: targeting influencers in a 1,000-student population could amplify effects, though real-world effectiveness depends on network structure and compliance.

However, the targeting paradox shows important limitations of this approach. Optimizing for average outcomes can actually make worst-case outcomes (burnout) worse, creating a trade-off between efficiency and equity. This finding aligns with critiques of targeted interventions in public health, where focusing only on high-risk individuals can overlook broader systemic effects on vulnerable populations (Frohlich & Potvin, 2008). Similarly, in our model, optimizing for average stress reduction through influencer targeting can unintentionally worsen outcomes for more vulnerable agents.

4.2 Mechanisms Underlying the Paradox

We attribute the paradox to the dual role of network hubs. Influencers act as stress amplifiers by broadcasting to many neighbors, but also serve as important sources of social connection and support. The intervention therefore creates asymmetric effects.

On one hand, reducing influencer activity lowers stress transmission across the network, decreasing average stress levels. On the other hand, it also reduces social interaction for their neighbors, leading to a loss of support. This loss of connection increases vulnerability among certain agents, pushing some of them past the burnout threshold.

As a result, while overall stress decreases, burnout cases increase due to these localized effects.

4.3 Implications for Campus Wellness Programs

Based on our simulation results, we suggest three directions that future programs might explore:

1. Monitor both outcomes: Our model shows that targeting influencers helps average stress but can increase burnout. Programs should track both.

2. Test your group: Conformity matters. When we tested highly conformist networks (β = 0.8), targeting worked much better (22% reduction). When networks were loose (β = 0.1), it barely helped. Different student groups may respond differently.

3. Consider what influencers provide: Our model suggests influencers provide support beyond just spreading stress. If they reduce usage, neighbors lose this connection. Future work might explore whether keeping their social ties active while reducing their stress sharing would help.

4.4 Limitations and Future Directions

Our work has several limitations. First, the stress dynamics are theoretical constructs based on literature rather than direct measurements of how stress actually spreads among students. We would need longitudinal data using methods like Experience Sampling to validate whether stress really spreads this way in student networks.

Second, we assume the network stays the same, but real friendships form and break over time. Real social networks change in ways that could make interventions work better or worse. Future work should test models where friendships change based on shared stress levels or who joins interventions.

Third, we model stress transmission as linear, but real contagion might require thresholds where you need multiple stressed friends before it affects you. Testing non-linear transmission could show when targeting strategies work better or worse.

Fourth, all our simulations use one network structure (seed=42). While we vary the random day-to-day noise across 5 seeds, we should test multiple different network structures with different clustering patterns to see if results hold up.

Finally, we assume perfect compliance with the intervention. Real adoption would face issues with motivation, long-term sustainability, and possible backlash that we don't model.

4.5 Broader Implications for Social Computing

This work shows that agent-based modeling can connect individual psychology with network dynamics, giving us a way to test intervention ideas computationally before trying them in the real world. The targeting paradox is a good example of how network interventions can have unexpected emergent effects that you wouldn't predict just by looking at individuals.

More broadly, our findings add to debates about how to design interventions in social systems. The fact that collective action worked 5 times better than targeted intervention (when peer influence is moderate) suggests that for many behavioral health problems, getting everyone to change a little might beat getting a few people to change a lot. This matches what's called the "prevention paradox" - small changes across a whole population often work better than big changes in high-risk groups.

But we also found this is context-dependent. When we increased peer influence to β = 0.8, targeting influencers became way more effective. This means there's no one-size-fits-all strategy. Good intervention design needs to understand both the network structure and how strongly people influence each other.

5. CONCLUSIONS

This study demonstrates that digital stress propagation can be effectively modeled using an agent-based framework that integrates real behavioral data with social contagion theory. Our simulations show that targeting network influencers produces measurable stress reduction (5.9%, p < 0.05) but reveals a key trade-off: improving average outcomes can make extreme cases worse. Getting everyone to participate works much better than targeting when peer influence is moderate, but targeting works more effectively in conformist groups (22% reduction with only 1 burnout).

Our model suggests that effective digital wellness strategies may require mixed approaches that combine network insights with equity considerations, with specific choices depending on the group context. Future work should test our assumptions using real data over time, try dynamic network models, and look at non-linear ways stress might spread.

The main contribution of this research is showing how computer simulations can reveal unexpected effects in social systems, providing computational insights to inform hypotheses about resource allocation for mental health interventions that warrant empirical testing. As digital technologies keep changing how people interact, we increasingly need approaches to wellbeing that account for networks.
