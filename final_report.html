1. What We Actually Built and Why

The Core Idea

During exam season, we noticed something interesting: when one person in our friend group started stressing about exams on WhatsApp or Instagram, suddenly everyone else started feeling that stress too. It was like stress was contagious, spreading from person to person through our social connections. This got us thinking—if stress really does spread like a disease, could we use the same strategies that work for disease control to reduce stress in a network?

That's where our project started. We wanted to build a simulation that could answer a specific question: If we identify the most well-connected people in a social network and help them reduce their stress and screen time, would that reduce stress for everyone else too?

Think of it like vaccination strategy—you don't need to vaccinate everyone if you vaccinate the right people. We wanted to see if the same logic would work for mental health interventions.

Why This Matters

Campus wellness programs have limited resources. They can't run individual therapy sessions for 500 students. But what if they could identify 25 key students and focus their efforts there? If stress really spreads through networks, helping those 25 people might indirectly help hundreds of others. That's the practical question we wanted to answer.

2. The Dataset: Starting with Real People

Finding Real Data

We didn't want to make up numbers or guess how people use their phones. We found a dataset on Kaggle called the "Valakhorasani User Behavior Dataset" that had real smartphone usage data from 700 actual users. The dataset included things like:

  • How many hours per day they used their phone
  • How many times they opened apps
  • How many notifications they got
  • Battery drain (which correlates with screen time)

The data was already cleaned, which saved us a lot of time. We spent a few days just exploring it, making scatter plots, looking at correlations, and trying to understand what patterns existed.

Finding the Five Types of Users

One of the first things we did was run K-Means clustering to see if people naturally grouped into categories. We tried different numbers of clusters (k=3, k=4, k=5, k=6) and looked at something called the silhouette score to see which made the most sense. K=5 gave us the best results.

What we found were five really distinct types of phone users:

User Type Daily Screen Time % of People Description
Minimalists ~1.5 hours 14% Use phone only when needed
Moderate Users ~3 hours 21% Normal, balanced usage
Active Users ~5 hours 31% Heavy but not problematic
Heavy Users ~7 hours 25% Getting into problematic territory
Digital Addicts ~10 hours 9% Phone is basically always on

These personas became the foundation for our simulation. Instead of having 100 identical agents, we could create agents that matched these real-world patterns.

3. Building the Simulation

Creating 100 Synthetic Agents

We needed 100 agents for our simulation (not too small to see network effects, not so large that it takes forever to run). We generated them by sampling from the distributions we found in the clustering. For example, if 14% of real users were Minimalists, then 14 of our 100 agents would be Minimalists too.

For each agent, we gave them:

  • A baseline screen time (from their persona)
  • A starting stress level (higher screen time = higher starting stress)
  • A resilience score between 0.7 and 1.3 (some people handle stress better than others)
  • A susceptibility score between 0.5 and 1.5 (some people are more influenced by their friends' stress)
  • A variability score between 0.8 and 1.2 (some people's daily habits are more consistent than others)

We validated that our synthetic agents actually matched the real dataset—the average screen time, app usage, etc. were all within 5% of the original data. This was important because we wanted our simulation to reflect reality, not just made-up numbers.

Building the Social Network

Here's where it got interesting. We couldn't just randomly connect people—that wouldn't match how real friendships work. In real life, people tend to be friends with similar people. Gamers hang out with gamers, heavy phone users hang out with other heavy users, etc. This is called "homophily."

We used what's called a Stochastic Block Model with these connection rules:

  • If two agents have the same persona: 15% chance they're friends
  • If they have different personas: 3% chance they're friends
  • Plus some random connections: 1% chance for diversity

This gave us a network with 100 people and 270 friendships, where the average person had about 5-6 friends. We calculated something called "assortativity" which was 0.426—this confirmed that our network successfully captured homophily (values near 0 would mean random connections, values near 1 would mean perfect clustering).

The Math: How Stress Spreads

This was probably the hardest part to get right. We needed equations that would make stress spread realistically through the network. After reading a bunch of papers (especially one by Christakis & Fowler from 2008 about how happiness spreads), we settled on these update rules:

Every Day, Two Things Happen:

1. Stress gets calculated based on three factors:

Stress = 0.6 × (Your Phone Usage) + 0.3 × (Average Friend Stress) × (Your Susceptibility) − 0.1 × (Your Resilience)

Let me break this down:

  • 60% comes from your own behavior. Your personal phone usage is the biggest factor. This makes sense—you have control over your own stress.
  • 30% comes from your friends. If all your friends are stressed, you absorb some of that. The Christakis paper found about 30% transmission rate for emotions, so we used that.
  • 10% protective effect from resilience. This acts as a buffer—some people just handle stress better.

2. Phone usage changes based on yesterday's stress (the feedback loop):

Today's Usage = Yesterday's Usage × (1 + 0.02 × Yesterday's Stress / 100) × Variability × Random Noise

This is modeling "doomscrolling." When you're stressed, you use your phone more to cope, which makes you more stressed, which makes you use your phone more... it's a vicious cycle. The 0.02 means that for every 100 points of stress, usage goes up by 2%. This number came from a 2011 study by Thomée about phone use and stress.

Why These Specific Numbers?

People always asked us during the poster presentation: "Why 0.6, 0.3, and 0.1? Why not 0.5, 0.4, 0.1?"

Honest answer: We tried different combinations. But we anchored on research:

  • Personal behavior should dominate (hence 0.6 being the largest)
  • Peer influence around 30% matched the social contagion literature
  • Resilience should help but not eliminate stress entirely

We also ran the simulation with different values and found that as long as personal usage was the dominant factor (bigger than peer influence), the results were qualitatively similar. It's not that 0.6 is magic—it's that the relative proportions matter.

4. The Intervention Experiment

What We Actually Tested

We ran two scenarios, each 5 times with different random seeds:

Scenario A - Baseline (No Intervention):

  • Just let the network run for 30 days naturally
  • This is our control group
  • Shows what happens without any intervention

Scenario B - Influencer Intervention:

  • On Day 10, we identify the 5 most connected people
  • We force their screen time down to 50% of what it would normally be
  • We also reduce how much stress they broadcast to their friends by 70%
  • Then we watch what happens over the remaining 20 days

Why Day 10?

We picked Day 10 because that's when stress has built up enough to matter, but there's still enough time left to see if the intervention works. We tested Day 5 and Day 15 too—Day 5 was too early (not much stress yet), and Day 15 worked but not quite as well.

Why 70% Transmission Reduction?

When someone reduces their phone usage, they're not just helping themselves—they're also posting less on social media, which means their friends see less of their stress. The 70% reduction models this double effect. The Christakis paper suggested that social contagion effects drop by about 60-70% per "hop" in the network, so we used that as our guide.

Finding the Influencers

We used something called "degree centrality"—basically, who has the most friends? Our top 5 influencers had between 13-18 connections each, while the average person had only 5-6. These were the super-connected people in our network.

5. Results: What Actually Happened

The Main Finding

Targeting just 5 people (5% of the network) reduced stress for everyone by 5.9%.

Final stress went from 42.61 down to 40.09, and we prevented about 0.4 burnout cases on average.

Metric Baseline With Intervention Change
Average Network Stress 42.61 ± 1.16 40.09 ± 0.73 −5.9%
People in Burnout (stress > 80) 5.8 ± 1.3 5.4 ± 1.1 −0.4 people
Days to Stabilize 23.4 21.8 1.6 days faster

This was consistent across all 5 runs, so it wasn't just random luck. The effect was real.

The Surprising Discovery: The Targeting Paradox

This was the most interesting thing we found, and honestly, we didn't expect it.

After getting our main results, we decided to test what would happen if we targeted different people. We ran three versions:

  • Version 1: Target the 5 most connected people (what we already did)
  • Version 2: Pick 5 random people
  • Version 3: Target the 5 least connected people
Strategy Final Stress Burnout Cases
Target Influencers (most connected) 40.09 (BEST) 3.60 (WORST)
Target Random People 41.00 3.40
Target Isolated People (least connected) 41.62 (WORST) 3.20 (BEST)

The Paradox: Targeting influencers gave us the lowest average stress BUT the highest number of burnout cases. Targeting isolated people gave us the highest average stress BUT the lowest burnout cases.

Why Does This Happen?

We spent a lot of time trying to understand this. Here's what we figured out:

When you "quarantine" an influencer (force them to reduce usage), two things happen:

  1. Good effect: They stop spreading stress to their 15+ friends → network average stress goes down
  2. Bad effect: Those friends lose an important social connection → some of them experience stress spikes → pushes vulnerable people over the burnout threshold

It's like removing the popular kid from school. Yeah, they might have been spreading some stress, but they were also a central social hub. When they go dark, everyone connected to them feels the loss.

This was a genuinely surprising finding. It means that optimizing for average stress is not the same as optimizing for preventing worst-case outcomes. If your goal is to prevent burnout (not just reduce average stress), targeting influencers might actually be the wrong strategy.

What If Everyone Participates? (Digital Detox Day)

One of the committee members asked us: "What if instead of targeting 5 people, you convinced everyone to participate?"

We ran this experiment after the poster presentation. If all 100 people reduce usage by 50% for one day:

  • Stress drops by 29% (vs. 6% when targeting influencers)
  • Burnout cases drop to 0.6 (vs. 5.4 baseline)

Collective action absolutely destroys targeted intervention. It's not even close. When everyone participates, there's no one left to spread stress—the entire contagion cycle breaks down.

But the catch is: getting 100% participation is way harder than convincing 5 people to change. That's the real-world trade-off.

6. What We Learned About Social Computing

Networks Are Not Intuitive

Before this project, I would have confidently said "obviously targeting influencers is the best strategy for everything." But the targeting paradox showed us that network interventions have trade-offs that aren't obvious. The most connected people aren't always the best intervention targets—it depends on whether you care about averages or outliers.

Individual vs. Collective Interventions

The fact that collective action beat targeted intervention by such a huge margin (29% vs. 6%) was eye-opening. It suggests that when peer influence is moderate (like in our model where it's 30%), network structure matters less than just getting everyone to change their behavior a little bit.

But there's a flip side: in our "echo chamber" scenario where we cranked peer influence up to 80%, targeting influencers became incredibly effective (65% stress reduction!). So the optimal strategy depends on how conformist your population is.

Feedback Loops Are Powerful

The doomscrolling feedback loop (stress → more usage → more stress) is subtle but important. Even with a small coefficient (0.02), over 30 days it creates this steady upward drift in both stress and usage. Breaking that cycle, even for just 5 people, propagates through the network.

7. Problems We Ran Into

The Seed Dependency Issue

This was a bug we discovered late. Our network topology was generated with a random seed, but we didn't lock that seed. So theoretically, if someone re-runs our code, they might get a different network structure and different "top influencers."

We fixed it by documenting that the network was generated with seed=42, and all our results use that specific network. But it's a reminder that reproducibility in stochastic simulations is tricky.

Choosing Parameters

The hardest part was honestly just deciding on values for all the parameters. We had research papers to guide us (the 0.3 peer influence came from Christakis & Fowler, the 0.02 feedback came from Thomée), but things like the resilience range [0.7, 1.3] were more arbitrary.

We did try different values and found that the results were qualitatively similar (intervention still worked, paradox still appeared), but the exact numbers changed. This is both reassuring (the findings are robust) and concerning (there's still some arbitrariness).

Validation

We validated that our synthetic agents matched the real dataset (<5% error), but we couldn't validate the stress dynamics because we don't have real longitudinal stress data from 100 students over 30 days. That would require an actual study, which we didn't have time or resources for.

So our model is empirically grounded in the usage data, but the stress propagation equations are more theoretical. That's a limitation.

8. What We Would Do Differently

If We Had More Time

  • Test different network structures: We only used one network topology (seed=42). Would the results hold on completely different network shapes?
  • Dynamic networks: Real friendships change over time. People make new friends, drift apart, etc. Our static network doesn't capture that.
  • Non-linear stress transmission: We assumed stress spreads linearly, but maybe there are threshold effects? Like you need to see 3 stressed friends before it affects you, not just 1?
  • Real-world validation: The dream would be to run this with actual Experience Sampling Method (ESM) data where you ping students throughout the day and ask "how stressed are you right now?"

Parameter Sensitivity Analysis

We should have done a systematic sweep of the parameters. What happens if we vary α from 0.4 to 0.8? Does the intervention still work? Does the paradox disappear? We did some informal testing but not a rigorous sensitivity analysis.

9. Real-World Applications

What This Actually Means for Campus Wellness

If you're running a university wellness program, our findings suggest:

  1. Don't rely solely on targeting influencers. It helps with average stress but can actually increase burnout cases. You need to monitor vulnerable individuals too.
  2. Collective campaigns are way more effective. A campus-wide "Digital Detox Wednesday" would probably work better than targeted interventions on 25 key students.
  3. But targeted is more feasible. Getting 100% participation is hard. So maybe the answer is hybrid: target influencers AND run population-wide awareness campaigns.
  4. It's never too late. Even late interventions (Day 25 of 30) still showed measurable effects. Don't give up just because it's Week 12 of the semester.

The Echo Chamber Insight

In highly conformist environments (high schools, tight-knit communities), targeting influencers becomes incredibly effective. So the optimal strategy depends on your population. Are they independent thinkers or highly conformist? That determines whether network-based interventions will work well.

10. Honest Limitations

Let's be clear about what this model can't do:

  • It's a simulation, not reality. We don't have real stress data from real students. We have real usage data, but the stress equations are our best guess based on literature.
  • Static network. Friendships don't change over 30 days in our model. Real social networks are dynamic.
  • Linear transmission. Stress might actually spread in more complex ways (thresholds, reinforcement, etc.).
  • Binary burnout. We defined burnout as stress > 80, but real burnout is a spectrum, not a switch.
  • One network topology. All our results come from one random network structure. Would they generalize?

That said, these limitations don't invalidate the findings—they just mean we should be careful about claiming this is exactly how stress works in real life. It's a model. All models are wrong, but some are useful.

11. What We're Proud Of

  • We used real data, not made-up numbers
  • We ran multiple simulations (5 per scenario) for statistical validation
  • We found a non-obvious result (the targeting paradox) that we didn't expect
  • We tested alternative scenarios after the fact (detox day, echo chamber, etc.)
  • The code is documented and reproducible

This project taught us that social computing is genuinely different from regular programming. You're not just writing code—you're modeling human behavior. And human behavior is weird, non-linear, and full of surprises.

12. Technical Implementation Notes

What We Actually Coded

The project is 7 Python scripts that run in sequence:

  1. data_profiling.py - Load and explore the 700-user dataset
  2. persona_definition.py - K-means clustering to find the 5 user types
  3. agent_factory.py - Generate 100 synthetic agents
  4. network_topology.py - Build the social network with homophily
  5. temporal_engine.py - Run the 30-day simulation (baseline)
  6. run_scenarios.py - Run intervention scenarios (5 times each)
  7. create_visualizations.py - Make all the graphs for the poster

Total runtime is about 60 seconds for everything. We used NetworkX for the graph stuff, scikit-learn for clustering, and pandas/numpy for everything else. Nothing fancy, just solid Python libraries.

The Hardest Code to Write

The temporal simulation loop was tricky because you have to be careful about update order. Do you update everyone's stress first, then usage? Or do them simultaneously? We chose to update stress based on yesterday's neighbor values (to avoid order-dependence), then update usage, then move to the next day.

13. Final Thoughts

This project changed how I think about digital wellness. Before, I thought of stress as an individual problem—if you're stressed, that's your issue to solve. But this simulation showed that stress operates at the network level. Your stress affects your friends, which affects their friends, which eventually comes back to affect you.

The policy implications are real. If stress is contagious, then individual therapy isn't enough. You need network-aware interventions. But as the targeting paradox showed, naive network interventions can backfire. You can't just target influencers and call it done—you have to think about how removing those central nodes affects the people who depend on them.

The most important lesson: collective action beats clever optimization. Getting everyone to participate even a little bit is way more effective than getting a few key people to change a lot. But collective action is hard. So maybe the answer is both: use network analysis to identify influencers, but combine that with population-wide campaigns. Hybrid strategies for hybrid problems.

Would love to run this on real longitudinal data someday. Until then, this simulation is our best attempt at understanding how stress spreads through the networks we all live in.
