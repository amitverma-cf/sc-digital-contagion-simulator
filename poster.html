Abstract

We developed a "Digital Twin" of a social network to analyze how stress spreads through peer interactions. Using behavioral data from 700 smartphone users, we trained 100 synthetic agents to mimic real human patterns. We simulated 30 days of interaction where stress spreads like a contagion. Our results show that targeting just the top 5% of highly connected users reduced the entire network's stress by 5.9%, though it revealed complex trade-offs regarding burnout prevention.

Methodology: The Digital Twin

1. Creating the Personas (K-Means Clustering)

We did not just make up the agents. We ran a K-Means clustering algorithm on the dataset to mathematically define 5 specific user types:

  • Minimalists (14%) - Low screen time, minimal app switching
  • Moderate Users (21%) - Balanced usage patterns
  • Active Users (31%) - Regular engagement
  • Heavy Users (25%) - High daily usage
  • Digital Addicts (9%) - Extreme usage, frequent notifications

2. Building the Network (Stochastic Block Model)

We constructed a social graph with 100 nodes and 270 edges. We used an "assortativity" factor of 0.426, which means the code forced agents to connect mostly with people who share their habits. This ensures the simulation mimics real-world social bubbles rather than a random group of strangers.

The Mathematical Model

Dynamic Stress Equation: We defined stress as a dynamic value that updates every single day based on this linear combination of three forces. Personal usage contributes directly (α=0.6), representing the primary driver of cognitive load. Peer influence (β=0.3) models the viral spread of stress through network neighbors, weighted by individual susceptibility (0.5-1.5, capturing variation in social conformity). Resilience (γ=0.1) provides a protective buffer based on the agent's individual coping stats (0.7-1.3 range). Stress is capped at 100.

Stresst = α·Usage + β·PeerStress·Suscept - γ·Resilience·10
α = 0.6 Personal usage weight β = 0.3 Peer influence weight γ = 0.1 Resilience dampening Suscept 0.5-1.5 (peer sensitivity) Resilience 0.7-1.3 (stress resistance)

The Feedback Loop: We coded a feedback mechanism (δ=0.02). If an agent gets stressed today, the model forces them to increase their app usage tomorrow. This simulates "doomscrolling," creating a vicious cycle where stress creates more usage, which creates more stress. Variability (0.8-1.2) captures behavioral consistency, while Gaussian noise (σ=0.1) adds realistic daily fluctuation. Usage floor is 30 min/day.

Usaget = Usaget-1 × (1 + δ·Stress/100) × Variability × Noise
δ = 0.02 Stress amplification Variability 0.8-1.2 (daily consistency) Noise N(1, 0.1) (random fluctuation)

Burnout Threshold: Stress > 80 (clinical significance based on standardized stress scales)

The Intervention Experiment

Targeting Method: We identified the Top 5 Influencers using Degree Centrality (the nodes with the highest number of connections). Alternative centrality measures (betweenness, eigenvector, pagerank) were tested; degree centrality proved most effective for this network topology (assortativity 0.426 favors local clustering).

Day 10-30: Digital Quarantine Protocol

  • Usage Clamp: Forced their screen time down by 50%
  • Transmission Block: Reduced the amount of stress they broadcast to neighbors by 70%
  • No change to resilience/susceptibility (isolates behavioral mechanism)

Rationale: Day 10 timing allows stress to build naturally before intervention, simulating real-world delayed response. Early intervention (Day 5) prevents meaningful stress accumulation; late intervention (Day 25) still achieves 7% reduction, demonstrating robustness.

Results & Findings

The Primary Numbers: Intervention reduced Day 30 population stress by 2.52 points (5.9% decrease, p<0.05). Error bars (±SD across 5 runs) show baseline variance (σ=1.16) higher than intervention (σ=0.73), indicating stabilizing effect. Statistical significance confirmed via paired t-test.

Interpretation: Burnout reduction modest (-0.4 agents, 6.9% decrease) due to threshold effects. Agents near 80 stress exhibit high variance. Faster stabilization (1.6 days) indicates intervention breaks stress-usage feedback loops earlier, preventing runaway dynamics.

Key Findings: The Complex Discoveries

Finding 1: The Targeting Paradox

This was our most complex discovery. Targeting influencers successfully lowered the average stress of the whole network, but it actually increased the number of burnout cases by 12.5% compared to targeting random people. The Reason: Influencers act as social hubs. When we "quarantine" them, their dependent friends lose social connection, causing localized stress spikes that push vulnerable agents into burnout.

Finding 2: The Echo Chamber Effect

We ran a separate test where we increased the peer influence weight to 80% (simulating a highly conformist environment). Without any intervention, high peer influence created a stress cascade that was 22% worse than low influence networks, resulting in persistent burnout cases. Real-world implication: Echo chambers amplify stress contagion, making highly connected networks more vulnerable to collective distress.

Finding 3: Collective Action vs. Leverage

We simulated a "Universal Detox" where all 100 agents reduced usage. This approach was 5 times more effective than targeting the influencers. This mathematically proves that while network leverage helps, it cannot outperform collective behavior change when contagion is weak (β=0.3).

Finding 4: Intervention Timing

Late intervention (Day 25 vs Day 10) still achieves 7% stress reduction and prevents 2 burnouts. System exhibits path dependence. Early stress establishes network gradients that persist. This proves late action can still disrupt equilibrium effectively.

Conclusion

What We Proved

  • Network Position: Intervening on just 5% of the network affected the stress levels of 100% of the agents through 2-3 hop diffusion
  • Trade-offs: We found that optimizing for lower average stress (targeting influencers) is different from optimizing for fewer burnouts (targeting isolated users)
  • Complexity: You cannot just treat the "super-spreaders" and expect the problem to vanish; the network structure fights back
  • Scale Matters: Universal interventions (100% coverage) outperform targeted strategies 5× in weak-contagion regimes (β=0.3)

Policy Implications & Future Directions

Campus wellness programs should adopt hybrid strategies that identify network influencers through centrality analysis while implementing population-level policies like screen-free hours. This combines topological efficiency with coverage completeness.

Limitations: Simplified linear stress transmission, static network topology, single intervention modality, synthetic agent population requiring real-world validation.

Future work: Non-linear contagion thresholds, dynamic network formation, combined interventions (detox + resilience training), calibration with longitudinal student health data.
